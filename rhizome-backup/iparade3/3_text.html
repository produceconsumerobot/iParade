Step Three

The builders visit the hackshop.

They have materials and purpose, schematics and memory chips.

Their firmware is regulated and, for efficiency, they have, until recently, subscribed to a direct upgrade plan.

But they must learn how to subvert the infrastructure, redirect outputs, extend and split the feed.

They come prepared with downloaded and printed plugs, beta versions of NeuroWare, which arrived with promotional stickers in semi-translucent aluminum bags.

A historian greets them at the doors with a reassuring voice.

The historian’s hands collect artifacts. Not merely the objects themselves but the occasions in which they were held.

She thinks about how but she does not ask why.

The Hackshop was built by a man and his son, and now is operated by a man and his father.

Over the years, the two men have held various positions in the Hackshop: inventors, mechanics, and archivists.

The historian makes herself useful in the work of preservation and presentation:

"...they used to...and then it would...was put this way...and then they turned..."

The machines work but production is halted.

"We are in the business of synthesizing sounds and smells. Our work is the theater of manufacturing, our workers’-hands and our stained shirts."

She has many recording devices at her desk:

Paper, pen, measuring tape, Polaroid camera, Dictaphone, T-square, abacus, calculator... Her interviews will assist in fabrication of their custom electric extensions.
